Designing with Semantics: Building Accessible HTML

September 30, 2025 · 5 min read
Written by Huỳnh Tuấn Anh

Why Semantics Matter

Semantic HTML gives meaning to your content. It helps search engines understand your page structure and enables assistive technologies to guide users through your site more effectively.

Instead of using endless <div> and <span> elements, semantic tags like <header>, <main>, <section>, and <footer> describe the role of content clearly — improving both accessibility and maintainability.

Examples of Semantic Elements

  • <article>: Independent content, like a blog post or comment.
  • <section>: Thematic grouping of content within a page.
  • <aside>: Supplemental info, like tips or related links.
  • <figure> and <figcaption>: For images with captions.
  • <nav>: For site or page-level navigation.

Accessibility Benefits

Screen readers use semantic tags to identify sections of a page — such as navigation, main content, or articles. This allows visually impaired users to jump directly to relevant content and understand the hierarchy of information.

In short, semantics = clarity. And clarity = usability.

Quick Tip

Always use headings (<h1>...<h6>) in logical order. Don’t skip levels — it helps accessibility tools create a proper document outline.
